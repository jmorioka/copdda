Risks of Medical Treatment vs. Lung Transplant

Risks of Medical Treatment Risks of Lung Transplant
It doesn't work for everyone. Even if you handle your medicines well and exercise, you could get into a bad situation or catch the flu, and that could lead to you spending a long time sick in the hospital, going to the hospital multiple times, or dying. You might not be a match for available donor lungs. Even if you are eligible to get a transplant, you might be unlucky and never get lungs that are a match.
There will still be times when you are short of breath. You have to take a lot of drugs (dozens per day), and some of them will make you sick or at risk of being sick in other ways.
You’ll still need oxygen. Though you might need a little less oxygen with the right medicine and exercise, you’ll still have to use it and carry it with you. The transplant process is stressful for you and even more stressful for your caregiver.
You'll still need to use inhalers and probably have to take other pills often to help with other problems that COPD creates in your body. Complications from the transplant could make you have to stay in the hospital for months or undergo painful medical treatments.
Your independence will still be limited. Depending on how sick you are, you may still need to depend on others to shop, cook, clean, drive, etc. for you. After the transplant you have to visit the transplant doctors regularly and get tests and you may have to live close to the hospital.
Lots of little things change: there’ll be foods you can’t eat, places you shouldn’t go, and things can’t do.
Transplant is expensive.
